CMB polarisation

Temperature anisotropies teach us a lot about the Universe. But to understand inflation, we need to look at another property of the CMB: its polarisation.

Basics of polarisation

(figure) Light is polarised when its electric field oscillates preferentially in a given direction

The CMB is polarised through Thomson scattering of photons off free electrons in the primordial plasma

Efficiency of Thomson depends on angle between incoming and outgoing polarisation directions

To characterise linearly polarised light, use Stokes parameters \(I\) \(Q\) and \(U\)

The same way as total intensity maps, we can make Q and U maps of the sky

  • Thomson scattering \(\propto \lvert \hat\epsilon \cdot \hat\epsilon' \rvert^2\)
  • 3 Stokes parameters entirely characterise linearly polarised light
    • \(I\) = total intensity
    • \(Q\) = difference in brightness between horizontal and vertical
    • \(U\) = difference in brightness between +45¬∞ and -45¬∞

\(E\) and \(B\) modes

Q and U -> recover polarisation field = headless vector

(figure) polarisation field on top of total intensity map from Planck

Q and U depend on coordinate system

Decomposition into \(E\) (parity-even) and \(B\) (parity-odd) patterns

\(E\) and \(B\) related to physical processes in the plasma

Remarkably, \(B\) modes are only sourced by tensor perturbations, GWs! (at first order)

By measuring CMB polarisation, specifically B modes, access to \(r\) and inflation

  • \(B\) modes are only sourced by tensor perturbations!
  • By measuring CMB polarisation, we have access to \(r\) and inflation

Current constraints on \(r\)

For now, no detection of primordial \(B\) modes has been made. We only have upper limits on the value of \(r\).

The best constraint to date is from the BICEP/Keck collaboration combined with Planck data.

The figure on the right shows measurements of \(B\)-mode power spectrum from various experiments.

Target either recombination bump (\(\ell \approx 100\)) or reionisation bump (\(\ell \lt 10\)).

In both cases we need a measurement of the CMB polarisation at intermediate to large scales.

Best: \(r < 0.032\) at 95% confidence

The Simons Observatory

Measuring \(r\) is one of the main science goals of the Simons Observatory.

International collaboration with 50+ institutes, 300+ members

The observatory is located in the Atacama desert in Chile, which is high and dry, ideal for millimetre-wave observations. SO builds on decades of experience in observing CMB from the ground.

It comprises three small aperture telescopes (SATs) dedicated to the measurement of large scale polarisation. A large aperture telescope (LAT) will observe smaller scales (high resolution) over a large fraction of the sky.

Very broad science case, from inflation to neutrino mass and number of relativistic species, etc. But the main goal for the SATs is to reach \(\sigma(r) = 0.002\).

To meet these ambitious goals, SO has deployed about 60,000 detectors over 6 frequency bands. Observations have started since last year, and we are analysing the data now!

  • Cerro Toco, Chile, 5200m altitude (‚Äúhigh and dry‚Äù)
  • 3 small (SATs) + 1 large aperture telescopes (LAT)
  • LAT for small scales over large sky fraction
  • SATs for polarisation at degree scales
    • baseline \(\sigma(r) = 0.003\) (goal \(0.002\))
  • 60,000+ detectors
  • 6 frequency bands, 27-280 GHz

From time-ordered data to maps

Start from figure on the right!

Lensing converts \(E\) -> \(B\) and vice versa.

CMB signal is faint: each individual data sample is noise dominated.

To achieve a good sensitivity across a significant sky area, CMB telescopes collect lost of measurements across the targeted area by scanning. This also helps to mitigate low-frequency instrumental noise.

The raw data is therefore made of time series (one/detector) called time-ordered data (TOD).

With knowledge of the scanning strategy (pointing information) we can reconstruct the observed sky maps -> map-making.

Map-making = key starting point of analysis pipeline

Must preserve all information + mitigate as many systematics as possible

map-making = from time-ordered data (TOD) to sky maps at observed frequencies

data reduction step: ~6 orders of magnitude

Linear data model

Formally: linear data model

Inverse problem ‚Äì what we recover/know/assume

Linear estimator = mapping of data to maps

\[ d = Ps + Tx + n \]

  • we know \(d\) (the data)
  • we estimate \(s\) (sky maps) and marginalize over \(x\) (spurious signals)
  • we model \(P\) (pointing) and \(T\) (templates)
  • we assume that \(n\) (noise) has covariance \(N\)

‚ÄúMap-making‚Äù = linear operation \(\hat s = L d\) to estimate sky maps from the data.

Unbiased estimators

Weighted co-addition of the data with spurious signals filtered out.

Generic linear unbiased estimator [Poletti et al. 2017] \[ \hat s = (P^\top F_T P)^{-1} P^\top F_T d \]

with filtering and weighting operator \[ F_T = W \left[\mathbb I - T (T^\top W T)^{-1} T^\top W \right] \]

  • filters out unwanted signals (columns of \(T\))
  • weights orthogonal modes by weight matrix \(W\)

Minimum variance (optimal) if \(W = N^{-1}\) (generalized least squares, GLS).

Practical considerations

  • Size of the problem
    • 1,000 detectors + months of data + sampling @100 Hz -> \(n_t \sim 10^{11}\) samples (TB scale)
    • resolution + sky coverage + Stokes (I)QU -> \(n_p \sim 10^5 - 10^8\) sky pixels
    • correlations -> process data in one go
    • Numerical efficiency and parallelism are key!
  • Noise correlations \(N\) (size \(n_t \times n_t\))
    • split data in chunks (trade-off efficiency/quality)
    • assume noise is stationary in each chunk, \(N(t,t')=N(\lvert t-t' \rvert)\)
    • characterise in Fourier space (power spectral density, PSD)
  • Iterative solvers
    • \(\hat s = A^{-1} b\) -> instead solve \(A s = b\)
    • store \(A\) implicitly without constructing the whole matrix
    • preconditioned conjugate gradient (PCG) algorithm
    • convergence speed influenced by noise correlations

Data: \(d = Ps + Tx + n\)

Pair differencing

Write down minimal model for one pair of detectors.

Use template filtering to estimate \(Q\) and \(U\), marginalising over \(x\).

Find pair differencing!

Minimal model: \(T \rightarrow \mathbb I\) \[ d = \begin{bmatrix} d^A \\ d^B \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbb I \\ \mathbb I \end{bmatrix} x + \begin{bmatrix} P_{QU} \\ -P_{QU} \end{bmatrix} s_{QU} + \begin{bmatrix} n^A \\ n^B \end{bmatrix} \]

GLS template filtering estimator gives \[ \hat s_{QU} = (P_{QU}^\top N_-^{-1} P_{QU})^{-1} P_{QU}^\top N_-^{-1} d_- \] with \(d_- \equiv d^A - d^B\) and \(N_-\) the covariance of \(n_- \equiv n^A - n^B\).

Minimal model equivalent to pair-differencing (PD).

Results: impact on \(r\)

How do these results translate in terms of \(r\) sensitivity?

Remarkably little impact given the very general assumption behind PD.

  • 25 realisations of realistic instrumental \(1/f\) noise
  • detector-dependent parameters (knee, slope, white level)
  • noise power spectrum \(N_\ell^{BB}\) from reconstructed maps \(\hat s^\mathrm{pd}_{QU}\)
  • Fisher forecast: \[ \sigma(r=0)^{-2} \approx \frac{f_\text{sky}}{2} \times \sum_{\text{bins $b$}} \Delta_\ell (2\ell_b+1) \Bigg( \frac{C_{\ell_b}^{BB,\text{prim}} \rvert_{r=1}}{C_{\ell_b}^{BB,\text{lens}} + \langle N_{\ell_b}^{BB} \rangle} \Bigg)^2 \]

Moderate increase of error bars for typical 10% variation of parameters.
